# Salary Negotiation Playbook

A comprehensive guide to negotiating job offers, including objection handling, email templates, and tactical advice.

## Core Principles

1. **Negotiation is expected.** Companies build negotiation room into offers. Not negotiating leaves money on the table.
2. **An offer is the beginning, not the end.** The company has decided they want you — that's your leverage.
3. **Total comp matters more than base.** Base + bonus + equity + benefits + perks = your actual compensation.
4. **Base compounds forever.** A $5K base increase is worth far more than a $5K signing bonus over a 3-year stay.
5. **You're not being greedy.** You're advocating for fair compensation for the value you bring.

## The Negotiation Sequence

### Step 1: Receive the Offer

**Never accept on the spot**, even if it's amazing.

Script: "Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity and the team. I'd like to take a day or two to review the full package carefully. Can you send the details in writing?"

### Step 2: Research

Before responding, research:
- Market rate for the role (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Payscale, LinkedIn Salary)
- The company's pay bands (often available through levels.fyi or internal networks)
- Cost of living if relocating
- Your walk-away number, target, and stretch

### Step 3: Build Your Case

Your leverage comes from:
- **Competing offers** (strongest leverage)
- **Market data** showing you're below range
- **Specialized skills** that are hard to find
- **The company's urgency** to fill the role
- **Your current comp** (if it's higher than the offer)

### Step 4: Counter

**On a call (preferred) or via email:**

Script: "I'm really excited about joining [Company]. After reviewing the offer and doing some research on the market, I was hoping we could discuss the compensation. Based on my experience in [specific area] and the market data I'm seeing for this level, I was targeting a base in the $X-Y range. Is there flexibility there?"

## Objection Handling Scripts

### "That's above our budget for this role."

**Response:** "I understand budget constraints. If the base is firm, are there other areas we could explore? I'm thinking about things like equity, signing bonus, or an accelerated review timeline."

**Backup:** "Would it help if we agreed to a performance review at 6 months with a defined path to the $X level? That way the increase is tied to demonstrated value."

### "We pay everyone at this level the same."

**Response:** "I appreciate the consistency in your compensation structure. Could we explore a signing bonus to bridge the gap, or an accelerated path to the next level? I'd also be interested in discussing the equity component."

### "This is our best and final offer."

**Response:** "I appreciate you being straightforward. Before I make my decision, can you walk me through the full package? I want to make sure I'm considering everything — bonus structure, equity vesting, benefits, and professional development budget."

**Note:** "Best and final" is sometimes a negotiation tactic and sometimes true. If you have competing offers, this is when to mention them.

### "What are you making now?" / "What's your salary expectation?"

**Response (deflect current salary):** "I'd prefer to focus on the value I'll bring to this role rather than my current compensation. Based on my research for this position and market, I'm targeting the $X-Y range."

**Response (if pressed):** "My current total compensation is around $X, but I'm looking at this opportunity based on the market rate for the role, which I understand to be in the $Y-Z range."

### "We can't match your competing offer."

**Response:** "I understand, and I want to be transparent that it's not just about the highest number. I'm genuinely more excited about [Company] because of [specific reason]. If we can get closer on compensation, I'd prefer to come here. What would it take to get to $X?"

### "The equity makes up for the lower base."

**Response:** "I appreciate the equity component. Can you help me understand the vesting schedule, strike price or current valuation, and what a realistic liquidity event looks like? I want to make sure I'm comparing apples to apples with my other options."

### "You don't have enough experience for that salary."

**Response:** "I understand the concern. Let me share why I believe my experience warrants this level: [specific accomplishments, specialized skills, unique value]. Would you be open to a 6-month performance review with a clear path to $X based on demonstrated results?"

### "We've already stretched for you."

**Response:** "I appreciate that, and I want to make sure we find something that works for both of us. If the base is truly at its ceiling, could we discuss [specific alternative — signing bonus, extra PTO, remote flexibility, professional development budget]?"

## What to Negotiate Beyond Base

| Component | Negotiability | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| Base salary | High | Compounds over time — prioritize this |
| Signing bonus | High | One-time cost for company, easy to approve |
| Equity/RSUs | Medium-High | Especially at pre-IPO companies |
| Annual bonus target | Low-Medium | Usually set by level/band |
| Start date | High | Extra time = extra rest or extra income from current job |
| Remote work / flexibility | Medium | Post-COVID, often very negotiable |
| Title | Medium | Especially important for future job searches |
| PTO / vacation | Low-Medium | Some companies have flexible policies |
| Professional development budget | Medium | Training, conferences, education |
| Relocation package | High | If applicable, significant value |
| Review timeline | Medium | 6-month review instead of annual = faster raise |

## Email Templates

### Counter-Offer Email

Subject: Re: [Role] Offer — Compensation Discussion

```
Hi [Recruiter/Hiring Manager],

Thank you again for the offer to join [Company] as [Role]. I'm genuinely
excited about the opportunity to [specific thing about the role or team].

After reviewing the full package and researching the market for this role,
I'd like to discuss the base salary. Based on my [X years of experience in
Y, specific skills, relevant accomplishments], and the market data I'm
seeing for [role level] in [location], I was hoping we could get closer to
$[target].

I'm confident in the value I'll bring to the team, and I want to make sure
we start on the right foot. I'm open to discussing this and flexible on how
we get there — whether through base, equity, signing bonus, or a combination.

Would you be available for a quick call this week to discuss?

Best,
[Your Name]
```

### Accepting After Negotiation

```
Hi [Recruiter],

Thank you for working with me on the compensation package. I'm happy to
accept the offer of [final terms] for the [Role] position.

I'm excited to join the team on [start date]. Please send over any
paperwork and I'll get it back to you promptly.

Looking forward to getting started!

Best,
[Your Name]
```

### Declining Gracefully

```
Hi [Recruiter],

Thank you so much for the offer and for taking the time to discuss the
compensation. After careful consideration, I've decided to go in a
different direction.

This was a genuinely difficult decision — I was impressed by [specific
positive thing about the company/team]. I hope our paths cross again in
the future.

Wishing you and the team all the best,
[Your Name]
```

## Timing Tips

- **Best time to negotiate:** After receiving the written offer, before signing
- **Response time:** 24-48 hours is professional. Ask for more time if needed.
- **Avoid negotiating on Friday afternoon** — give them time to consult internally
- **If you have a deadline from another company**, share it (creates urgency)
- **Don't rush** — a few extra days of negotiation won't cost you the offer

## Common Mistakes

1. **Accepting immediately** out of excitement or fear
2. **Anchoring too low** by sharing your current (lower) salary
3. **Negotiating only base** and ignoring equity, bonus, and benefits
4. **Being adversarial** instead of collaborative ("How can we make this work?")
5. **Making ultimatums** without being willing to walk away
6. **Not having a backup plan** — competing offers give you real leverage
7. **Feeling guilty** — companies negotiate all the time; this is normal
8. **Negotiating over email when a call would be better** — tone is easier on a call
