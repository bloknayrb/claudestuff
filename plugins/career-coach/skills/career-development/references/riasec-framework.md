# Holland RIASEC Framework

The Holland Occupational Themes (RIASEC) model, developed by psychologist John Holland, proposes that both people and work environments can be classified into six types. Career satisfaction increases when there's alignment between a person's type and their work environment.

## The Six Types

### R — Realistic (The Builder)

**Core traits:** Practical, hands-on, physical, mechanical, straightforward
**Work style:** Prefers concrete problems, tools, machines, physical activity
**Values:** Tangible results, competence, independence
**Avoids:** Ambiguity, excessive social interaction, abstract theory

**Sample careers:** Engineer, Mechanic, Electrician, Architect, Surgeon, Pilot, Forester, Athletic Trainer, Construction Manager, Chef, Firefighter

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you prefer working with your hands or physical objects?
- Would you rather fix something than discuss it?
- Do you enjoy outdoor or physical work?

### I — Investigative (The Thinker)

**Core traits:** Analytical, intellectual, curious, independent, precise
**Work style:** Prefers research, data, abstract problems, working alone or in small groups
**Values:** Knowledge, competence, logical thinking, independence
**Avoids:** Sales, persuasion, repetitive tasks, leading others

**Sample careers:** Scientist, Data Analyst, Researcher, Software Developer, Economist, Physician, Psychologist, Pharmacist, Mathematician, Market Research Analyst

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you enjoy solving complex puzzles or problems?
- Would you rather analyze data than manage people?
- Are you drawn to understanding how things work at a deep level?

### A — Artistic (The Creator)

**Core traits:** Creative, expressive, intuitive, nonconforming, imaginative
**Work style:** Prefers unstructured environments, self-expression, original work
**Values:** Creativity, aesthetics, independence, originality
**Avoids:** Rigid rules, repetitive tasks, conventional environments

**Sample careers:** Graphic Designer, Writer, Musician, Art Director, UX Designer, Photographer, Interior Designer, Actor, Film Director, Marketing Creative

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you need creative expression in your work?
- Are you comfortable with ambiguity and open-ended problems?
- Do structured, rule-heavy environments feel stifling?

### S — Social (The Helper)

**Core traits:** Empathetic, cooperative, supportive, warm, responsible
**Work style:** Prefers helping, teaching, counseling, working with people
**Values:** Service, fairness, relationships, community
**Avoids:** Mechanical tasks, isolation, competitive environments

**Sample careers:** Teacher, Counselor, Social Worker, Nurse, HR Specialist, Physical Therapist, Community Manager, Nonprofit Director, Chaplain, Mediator

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you get energy from helping others?
- Would you choose a meaningful role over a high-paying one?
- Do you naturally take on the "listener" role in groups?

### E — Enterprising (The Persuader)

**Core traits:** Ambitious, energetic, confident, persuasive, competitive
**Work style:** Prefers leading, selling, influencing, managing, taking risks
**Values:** Achievement, status, power, financial reward
**Avoids:** Scientific analysis, routine work, following rather than leading

**Sample careers:** Sales Manager, Entrepreneur, Lawyer, Marketing Director, Real Estate Agent, Financial Advisor, Recruiter, Lobbyist, Executive, Consultant

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you enjoy persuading or influencing others?
- Are you comfortable taking charge and making decisions?
- Does competition motivate you?

### C — Conventional (The Organizer)

**Core traits:** Detail-oriented, organized, systematic, reliable, efficient
**Work style:** Prefers structured tasks, clear expectations, data and systems
**Values:** Accuracy, stability, order, efficiency
**Avoids:** Ambiguity, unstructured environments, creative risk-taking

**Sample careers:** Accountant, Actuary, Database Administrator, Auditor, Technical Writer, Logistics Coordinator, Compliance Officer, Paralegal, Bookkeeper, Quality Analyst

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Do you enjoy organizing information and systems?
- Do you prefer clear instructions over open-ended tasks?
- Are you drawn to precision and accuracy?

## Reading Your Holland Code

Your Holland code is a 3-letter combination of your top types (e.g., "IEA"). The order matters:

1. **Primary type** (first letter) — Your dominant orientation
2. **Secondary type** (second letter) — Your supporting strength
3. **Tertiary type** (third letter) — Additional influence

### Interpreting Combinations

Adjacent types on the hexagon (R-I, I-A, A-S, S-E, E-C, C-R) are more compatible. Opposite types (R-S, I-E, A-C) create interesting tensions that can be strengths in the right role.

**The RIASEC Hexagon:**
```
        R
      /   \
    C       I
    |       |
    E       A
      \   /
        S
```

## Using RIASEC for Career Exploration

1. **Identify your code** using self-assessment or the scoring tool
2. **Research careers** that match your top 2-3 types
3. **Evaluate your current role** — does it align with your code?
4. **Explore adjacent types** — careers at the intersection of your types are often the best fit
5. **Consider environment** — even within a career, different companies have different type cultures

## Limitations

- RIASEC captures interests, not abilities — you can be interested in something you're not yet skilled at
- Types are not fixed — they can shift with experience and life stage
- Cultural context matters — career norms vary by country and culture
- The model works best as an exploration tool, not a definitive classification
- No type is better than another — each has valuable contributions
