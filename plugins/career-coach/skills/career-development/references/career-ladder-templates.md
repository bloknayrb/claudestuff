# Career Ladder Templates

Career ladders describe progression paths within an organization. Understanding them helps you set realistic goals, prepare for promotions, and decide between IC (Individual Contributor) and management tracks.

## Individual Contributor (IC) Ladder

### Junior / Associate (Level 1-2)

**Focus:** Learning and execution under guidance
**Scope:** Individual tasks and small features
**Skills expected:**
- Core technical/domain skills
- Takes direction well
- Asks good questions
- Delivers assigned work reliably
- Growing independence over time

**Typical timeline:** 0-3 years experience

### Mid-level (Level 3)

**Focus:** Independent execution with increasing ownership
**Scope:** Full features, small projects, mentoring juniors
**Skills expected:**
- Works independently on well-defined problems
- Contributes to design decisions
- Writes clear documentation
- Mentors junior team members informally
- Identifies and flags risks

**Typical timeline:** 2-5 years experience

### Senior (Level 4)

**Focus:** Technical leadership and cross-team influence
**Scope:** Large features, system design, team-level impact
**Skills expected:**
- Drives technical decisions for their area
- Mentors multiple team members
- Identifies problems before they're assigned
- Balances technical debt with delivery
- Communicates effectively with non-technical stakeholders
- Considered a "go-to" person for their domain

**Typical timeline:** 5-10 years experience

### Staff / Principal (Level 5-6)

**Focus:** Organization-wide technical strategy
**Scope:** Multi-team architecture, technical vision, industry influence
**Skills expected:**
- Sets technical direction across teams
- Makes decisions with multi-year impact
- Navigates organizational complexity
- Represents the company externally (conferences, open source, etc.)
- Identifies opportunities at the intersection of technology and business
- Influences without authority across org boundaries

**Typical timeline:** 8-15+ years experience

### Distinguished / Fellow (Level 7+)

**Focus:** Industry-level impact and company-wide strategy
**Scope:** Company-wide or industry-wide
**Skills expected:**
- Shapes industry standards and practices
- Advises executive leadership on technical strategy
- Recruits and retains top talent by reputation
- Solves problems that define the company's competitive advantage

**Note:** These roles exist at large companies. Most careers peak at Senior or Staff, which is not a failure — it's the natural ceiling for most organizations.

## Management Ladder

### Team Lead / Tech Lead

**Focus:** First-line leadership, still hands-on
**Scope:** One team (4-8 people)
**Skills expected:**
- Balances individual contribution with team coordination
- Runs effective 1:1s and team meetings
- Provides constructive feedback
- Shields team from unnecessary interruptions
- Makes hiring recommendations

**Key transition:** From "best individual performer" to "person who makes the team perform"

### Manager

**Focus:** People management and team delivery
**Scope:** One team (5-12 people), full people management responsibility
**Skills expected:**
- Hiring, firing, performance management
- Career development for direct reports
- Sprint/project planning and delivery
- Stakeholder management
- Budget awareness
- Conflict resolution

### Senior Manager / Director

**Focus:** Multi-team strategy and organizational design
**Scope:** 2-5 teams, 15-50 people
**Skills expected:**
- Organizational design (team structure, roles, responsibilities)
- Cross-team coordination and dependency management
- Strategic planning (quarterly/annual)
- Executive communication
- Budget ownership
- Builds management capability in others

### VP / SVP

**Focus:** Department or division-level strategy
**Scope:** Multiple departments, 50-200+ people
**Skills expected:**
- Business strategy alignment
- P&L responsibility
- Executive leadership team participation
- Company-wide policy and culture influence
- External partnerships and representation

## IC vs Management Decision Framework

### The Skill-Will Matrix

Before deciding, assess honestly:

|  | **High Will** | **Low Will** |
|--|---|---|
| **High Skill** | Natural fit — go for it | Capable but unmotivated — explore why |
| **Low Skill** | Motivated but needs development — invest in growth | Wrong direction — reconsider |

### Questions to Ask Yourself

**Signals you might enjoy management:**
- Do you get energy from helping others succeed?
- Are you comfortable with ambiguity and context-switching?
- Can you handle difficult conversations (feedback, termination)?
- Do you enjoy organizational strategy and planning?
- Are you okay with slower, less visible personal output?

**Signals you might prefer IC:**
- Do you get deep satisfaction from hands-on work?
- Does meeting-heavy scheduling drain you?
- Do you prefer depth over breadth?
- Are you energized by solving technical problems directly?
- Do you want to stay current in your craft?

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Management is the only path to senior roles" | IC tracks go to Staff/Principal/Distinguished at top companies |
| "Managers make more money" | At most tech companies, IC and management pay is comparable at equivalent levels |
| "You can always go back to IC" | True, but your technical skills may atrophy — plan for this |
| "Good ICs make good managers" | Different skills entirely — technical excellence doesn't predict management ability |
| "Management means no more coding" | Depends on level and company — Team Leads often still code 30-50% |

### The Pendulum Approach

You don't have to choose permanently. Many successful careers alternate:

1. Start as IC, build deep expertise
2. Try management for 2-3 years
3. Return to IC with broader perspective (or stay in management)
4. Re-evaluate every few years

The experience of managing makes you a better IC, and deep IC experience makes you a better manager. Both are valuable.

## Using These Ladders

1. **Identify your current level** honestly (not aspirationally)
2. **Read the next level's expectations** — what gaps do you see?
3. **Build evidence** for the next level in your current role (don't wait for promotion to start acting at the next level)
4. **Have the conversation** with your manager about your growth plan
5. **Set a timeline** — if you're not progressing toward your goal, examine why
